# How Do Pilots Communicate?
## Modulation
Air traffic communications use <SmartLink slug="am">amplitude modulation</SmartLink> even though most radio communications today use [frequency modulation](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modulation_de_fr%C3%A9quence#:~:text=En%20modulation%20de%20fr%C3%A9quence%2C%20l,(att√©nuation%20et%20bruit%20importants).).  
While FM generally delivers better signal quality, it's more complex and requires hardware that‚Äôs more power-hungry and heavier‚Äîa real concern in aviation üõ©Ô∏è.  
Plus, **AM** is way more robust than FM, meaning the signal holds up better against obstacles like bad weather, mountains, and large buildings. This lets the transmission reach farther distances.

## Polarization
Air traffic communications use **vertical polarization**. This refers to the direction of the electric field in an electromagnetic wave. [This site](https://culturesciencesphysique.ens-lyon.fr/ressource/simu-polarisation.xml) explains the various types of polarization nicely.  
Simply put, a vertically polarized wave should be picked up by an antenna oriented **vertically**. Otherwise, you risk losing a large portion of the signal.

# Time to Listen
In my case, I use [SDR++](https://www.sdrpp.org) because I like the software, but any SDR software works similarly.  
- First, plug in your **SDR** receiver connected to your antenna.  
- Set an appropriate gain and choose **AM** as the modulation mode.  
- Also, adjust the **bandwidth** to around `15000` Hz.  
Once that‚Äôs done, you can either manually select a frequency you found on [Openaip](https://www.openaip.net) or simply monitor the spectrogram for any visible peaks.

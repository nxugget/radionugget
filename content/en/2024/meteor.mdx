# Who Are the METEOR?
The **METEOR** satellites started as Soviet, then Russian weather satellites. Since **1964**, about **70** models have been launched. For this article, we‚Äôre focusing only on the latest series, **METEOR-M**, and here‚Äôs a brief history:

![METEOR-M](/images/blog/2024/meteor/meteor2.svg)

Like their NOAA counterparts, the METEOR-M series orbits at roughly **800km** altitude and follows a **polar** orbit, more precisely, a **sun-synchronous** orbit. That means they always face the **Sun** üåû. For more information on orbit types, check out <SmartLink slug="orbits">this lesson</SmartLink>.

![Orbite polaire](/images/blog/2024/type-orbits/type-orbits6.svg)
A sun-synchronous orbit means the satellite passes over the same locations at the same [solar time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temps_solaire) **twice a day**.  
The main difference from NOAA is in the transmission mode: METEOR-M uses [LRPT](https://www.sigidwiki.com/wiki/Low_Rate_Picture_Transmission_(LRPT)), whereas NOAA used [APT](https://www.sigidwiki.com/wiki/Automatic_Picture_Transmission_(APT)).  
For this article, we‚Äôre interested in two METEOR-M satellites:

![METEOR-M N2 satellite](/images/blog/2024/meteor/meteor1.svg)
Note that **METEOR M2-4** is still in a testing phase. Its frequency sometimes fluctuates between `137.1` and `137.9MHz`, and its symbol rate varies between **72** and **80Kbit/s**.  
To verify its status, you can use [this site](https://usradioguy.com/meteor-satellite/), which shows the current state of the satellite as of **09/02/2024**.

# Time to Listen
On the antenna side, I use a <SmartLink slug="qfh">QFH</SmartLink> for `137MHz`. It features **left-hand circular polarization**, ideal for METEOR since they transmit with **right-hand circular polarization**.
For reception, we use [SatDump](https://www.satdump.org/download/). I won‚Äôt go into all the configuration details (I‚Äôve already done a tutorial on **SatDump** [here](https://www.satdump.org/download/)), but here‚Äôs the gist:
- First, using the `Tracking` section in SatDump or via [this website](https://www.n2yo.com/?s=33591), check when the satellite will pass by. This section also shows the satellite's current **elevation**.
  
  ![SatDump Tracking](/images/blog/2024/meteor/meteor5.png)
  
- In the `Device` section, select your **SDR** receiver, in my case, an [SDRPlay RSP1B](https://www.passion-radio.fr/recepteurs-sdr/rsp1-b-2669.html), then hit **Start** to begin recording.
- I recommend adjusting the `FFT Min` and `FFT Max` values in the `FFT` section: set `FFT Min` low enough that noise is barely visible, and then set `FFT Max` to `FFT Min + 30`.

  ![SatDump METEOR](/images/blog/2024/meteor/meteor4.png)
  
- In the `Processing` section, choose **METEOR M2-x LRPT 72k**. Set `Primary` to `137.900MHz` and `Backup` to `137.100MHz`.  
Click **Start** again to begin the decoding process. If everything is set correctly, you should see the signal from **METEOR M2-4** üõ∞Ô∏è!
To ensure the decoding is going smoothly, check that:
  - The SNR (displayed at the bottom) is ideally **10** or above. Even if it‚Äôs **>2**, you should still see something.
  - The `Viterbi` section at the bottom-right is all green, indicating good performance. üòÑ

  ![SatDump](/images/blog/2024/meteor/meteor6.png)

‚ö†Ô∏è Once the satellite has passed, click **Stop** in the `Processing` section before stopping the recording in the `Device` section. Reversing this order might cause you to lose the recorded data.

After the pass, wait a few moments while **SatDump** finishes decoding everything, then switch over to the `Viewer` tab to see your images and apply post-processing.

![SatDump Viewer](/images/blog/2024/meteor/meteor7.png)
You can tweak various settings to get false-color or infrared images:

![METEOR M2-4 Images](/images/blog/2024/meteor/meteor8.jpg)

The image quality is really impressive compared to NOAA‚Äôs.
